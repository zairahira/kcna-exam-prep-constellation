## Monolithic architecture

A **monolith** is a single deployable unit. The entire application - UI,
business logic, data access - is built, tested, and deployed as one
artifact (a single binary, a single WAR file, a single container).

### Advantages

- **Simple to develop** - one codebase, one build, one deploy.
- **Simple to test** - end-to-end tests run against one process.
- **Simple to debug** - one set of logs, one process to trace.
- **Low operational overhead** - one thing to monitor, scale, and deploy.

### Disadvantages

- **Scaling is all-or-nothing** - you can't scale just the part that's
  under load. The whole monolith scales together.
- **Deploys are risky** - a small change requires redeploying everything.
  One bug can take down the whole app.
- **Team coupling** - large teams working on the same codebase step on
  each other. Merge conflicts and coordination overhead grow fast.
- **Technology lock-in** - the whole app uses one language, one framework,
  one database.


## Microservices architecture

A **microservices architecture** decomposes the application into small,
independently deployable services. Each service owns one bounded piece of
business logic, has its own data store, and communicates with other services
over the network.

### Advantages

- **Independent scaling** - scale the services that need it, not everything.
- **Independent deploys** - ship one service without redeploying others.
  Smaller blast radius.
- **Team autonomy** - each team owns a service end-to-end. They pick their
  own language, framework, and release cadence.
- **Fault isolation** - one service crashing doesn't necessarily bring down
  the whole system (if properly designed).

### Disadvantages

- **Distributed systems complexity** - network failures, latency, partial
  failures, eventual consistency.
- **Operational overhead** - many services to deploy, monitor, log,
  trace. Requires mature CI/CD, observability, and orchestration.
- **Data management** - each service owning its data means no simple JOINs.
  You need patterns like event sourcing or saga for cross-service
  transactions.
- **Testing** - integration testing across services is harder than testing
  a single process.


## When to use which

| Situation | Recommended approach |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Small team, early product | Monolith - keep it simple |
| Need to ship fast and iterate | Monolith - less coordination overhead |
| Team has grown, deploys are slow and risky | Consider decomposing into microservices |
| Different parts have very different scaling needs | Microservices help here |
| Regulatory isolation between components | Microservices with clear boundaries |

A common pattern is to **start monolith, decompose later** when the team
and the product complexity justify it. Premature decomposition into
microservices is a well-documented antipattern.


## Communication patterns

Microservices communicate over the network. Two main styles:

### Synchronous (request/response)

- **REST** (HTTP/JSON) - simple, widely supported, human-readable.
- **gRPC** (HTTP/2, Protocol Buffers) - faster, strongly typed, better for
  service-to-service calls. CNCF Incubating project.

### Asynchronous (event-driven)

- **Message queues** (RabbitMQ, Amazon SQS) - one service publishes a
  message, another consumes it.
- **Event streaming** (Apache Kafka, NATS) - events are published to a
  log/stream, multiple consumers can read independently.

Async patterns reduce coupling - the sender doesn't wait for the receiver -
but add complexity (eventual consistency, message ordering, dead letters).


## The Kubernetes connection

Kubernetes doesn't require microservices, and microservices don't require
Kubernetes. But they're a natural fit:

- Each microservice runs as its own `Deployment` with its own replica count.
- `Services` provide stable network identities for inter-service
  communication.
- Scaling one service independently is trivial (change `replicas` or use
  an HPA).
- Rolling updates let you deploy one service without touching others.
